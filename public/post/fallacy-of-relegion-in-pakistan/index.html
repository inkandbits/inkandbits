The Fallacy of Relegion in Pakistan

Posted by Rowail Qaisar on Saturday, January 25, 2025

Fallacy of Relegion in Pakistan

Pakistan holds a distinctive place in history as the only nation that achieved independence primarily on religious grounds. Established on August 15, 1947, it was founded on the principles of Islam. However, from its inception, the country has grappled with defining its true identity, with religion playing a complex and often ambiguous role in its development.

At the time of independence, the ideological and philosophical direction of Pakistan was uncertain. Muhammad Ali Jinnah, the nation’s founder, had lived most of his life as a non-practicing Muslim. While he advocated for the establishment of a Muslim-majority state, his vision for Pakistan did not align with religious extremism. In his notable address on August 11, Jinnah articulated a secular vision for the country, emphasizing that citizens of all faiths should enjoy equal rights and opportunities.

This was the only occasion he explicitly referenced secularism, suggesting his belief that governance should not be dictated solely by religious tenets. Ishtiaq Ahmed, a noted scholar, has explored Jinnah’s intricate journey and his efforts to balance his vision for Pakistan with the pressures from religious factions. Over time, Jinnah’s early notions of secularism were increasingly overshadowed by the rising tide of Islamic fundamentalism. Religious parties and leaders, many of whom were instrumental in Pakistan’s creation, began advocating for an Islamic state governed by Islamic law, influencing the political and social landscape. The tension between Jinnah’s secular aspirations and the reality of Pakistan’s religious evolution has significantly influenced the nation’s path. As time passed, religion became a means for political leaders to strengthen their authority, with various governments leveraging Islam to legitimize their rule. This resulted in a skewed interpretation of religion being embedded in Pakistan’s legal and political frameworks, where religious orthodoxy often overshadowed the pluralistic values that Jinnah envisioned.
